Problem 1

a) Transparent Statistical Methods

In part 1, my coworkers used a linear regression to test whether precipitation predicts flooded wetland area. In part 2, my coworkers used a Kruskal-Wallis test to determine whether the median flooded wetland area differs across 5 water year classifications.

b) Figure Needed

An appropriate figure to accompany part 2 of the report would be a box and jitter plot to show the median and overall spread across the water year classifications. The x-axis/group should be water year classification (wet, above normal, below normal, dry, and critical drought), and the y axis should be flooded wetland area in m^2. Each classification should have its own custom color to ensure data is clearly displayed.

c) Suggestions for Rewriting

Based on our model predictions, we found that precipitation does not predict flooded wetland area (m^2)(Linear Regression, F(1, df) = f-statisic, R2 = R^2 value, p = 0.11, \(\alpha\) = significance level). We did not find a difference ( \(\eta^2\) = effect size) in median flooded wetland area (m2) between water year classifications (wet, above normal, below normal, cry, critical drought) (Kruskal-Wallis test, \(\chi^2\) (df) = H-statistic, p = 0.12, \(\alpha\) = significance level).

d) Interpretation

One additional variable that could impact the flooded wetland area of the San Joaquin River Delta is water management. As there is not sufficient evidence of precipitation having an effect on flooded wetland area in this study, it is likely that the management of the water is a stronger predictor than the amount of water itself. This could be tested by measuring the flooded wetland area under different water management strategies to determine which strategies may increase flooded wetland area, maximizing waterfowl breeding success.

Problem 3

a) Response Variable

In this dataset, the 0s and 1s in the binshrub and binsubshrub columns indicate whether there is shrub or sub-shrub cover in a nest-site. A 0 indicates no shrub or sub-shrub cover and a 1 indicates there is shrub or sub-shrub cover in the nest-site.

b) Purpose of Study

Visual Obstruction:

Visual obstruction is a continuous variable measured at the nest, and at 5 m and 10 m from the nest. As visual obstruction increases, the probability of Mountain Plover nest site use would decrease because mountain plovers have a positive response to sites with a higher percentage of bare ground (the least visual obstruction) and a negative response to increasing maximum vegetation height, and presence of shrub cover and cactus cover (higher visual obstruction) according to the nest-selection subsection under the results section.

Distance to Prairie Dog Colony Edge:

Distance to prairie dog colony edge is a continuous variable. As distance to the colony edge increases, the probability of a Mountain Plover nest site would decrease. According to the 2nd and 3rd paragraphs in the Discussion section of the paper, this may be due to complementary resources outside of the colony. It may be easier for adult plovers to travel with chicks if they are closer to the edge of the colony rather than at the center. Additionally, mountain plover density was highest in colonies that were 100-500 ha in size and low in larger colonies according to Figure 2 in the Results section, which could be related to distance from the edge as plovers would have to travel much further distances in very large colonies. I believe mountain plovers prefer nest sites that offer protection while still allowing for easy travel to outside resources, causing density to be the highest near the edges of moderately sized colonies.

d) Run the Models

mopl_clean <- mopl |> # create new dataframe
  clean_names() |> # clean names
  mutate( used_bin = case_when( # create new column used_bin from id column
    id == "used" ~ "1",
    id == "available" ~ "0"
  )) |> 
  select(used_bin, edgedist, vo_nest) # select variables
# model 0: null model
model0 <- lm(used_bin ~ 1,
             data = mopl_clean)
# model 1: all predictors
model1 <- lm(used_bin ~ vo_nest + edgedist,
             data = mopl_clean)
# model 2: visual obstruction
model2 <- lm(used_bin ~ vo_nest,
             data = mopl_clean)
# model 3: distance to edge of colony
model3 <- lm(used_bin ~ edgedist,
             data = mopl_clean)

e) Select the Best Model
